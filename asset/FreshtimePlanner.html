01
Project Overview

The Problem We Needed to Solve

Food waste is a $1 trillion global problem — much of it caused by unseen freshness loss across supply chains. Freshtime Planner was designed to help teams predict and visualize product freshness before execution, turning complex operational data into clear decisions. By enabling brands to validate journeys from farm to consumer in advance, the platform helps reduce waste, protect quality, and drive smarter supply-chain planning.

🎯

Goal

Enable brand owners to simulate and validate product freshness at every business steps, ensuring trips stay within their freshness budget before execution.

👥

Users

Brand owners, quality assurance teams, and supply-chain planners who need to verify process viability before pilot or rollout.

⚙️

Platform

Modern web application with a scalable backend architecture. Operational data is preloaded so teams can run simulations without setup friction.

📐

My Scope

End-to-end design: research, information architecture, UX flows, component system, and 40+ production screens.

🔥 The Problem

Brand owners had no tool to validate freshness viability before committing to a trip or process. Decisions were made reactively — after spoilage or quality failure — rather than proactively. Without a way to simulate cumulative time and temperature exposure across each business step, teams could not know whether a plan would exceed their quality thresholds until it was too late.

✅ The Solution

Planner loads operational data in advance and calculates cumulative freshness points consumed at each business step — based on time and temperature exposure — against predefined quality standards. Teams can adjust step parameters, see the impact instantly, and confirm a trip stays within budget before any pilot begins. Simulation replaces guesswork.

Design Process

How We Got Here

A structured design process that moved from discovery through system design to final delivery, with continuous validation at each stage.

Phase 01 — Discovery
Research & Problem Definition
We conducted stakeholder interviews with food producers, logistics managers and QC teams to understand how they currently track freshness. Key finding: most users relied on spreadsheets and gut instinct, with no scientific model behind decisions. We mapped existing workflows to identify friction points and opportunities for automation.
Stakeholder Interviews Workflow Mapping Decision Point Analysis Jobs-to-be-Done
Phase 02 — Definition
Information Architecture & Core Concepts
We defined the core mental model by mapping the supply chain journey into sequential business steps — from harvest to delivery — where each business steps consumes Freshtime Points, a freshness points based on time and temperature exposure. This abstraction translated complex Arrhenius science into a simple currency metaphor that non-scientists could easily understand, enabling teams to quickly assess whether a planned process stays within quality limits.
Freshness Budget Model Information Architecture Quality Threshold Design Data Modelling
Phase 03 — Design System
Component Library & Visual Language
Before designing screens, we established the design system: color palette rooted in Infratab's brand teal with a functional orange for warnings/actions, typography pairing, a set of reusable components (the planner table, donut, chart component, step timeline), and interaction patterns for inline editing, tab navigation, and contextual menus. The system ensured consistency across all screens.
Color System Data Visualization Interaction Patterns Component Library
Phase 04 — Screen Design
Screens Across All User Journeys
With the system in place, we designed all screens starting from the core planner view and radiating outward. The chart section received special attention with two distinct chart types (Reference and Planner) and multiple filter modes. We designed multiple states for key views: read mode, inline parameter editing, alerts warning, shared plan permissions, and the guest demo mode — ensuring the tool was equally usable by authenticated brand teams and prospective users exploring before sign-up.
charts Parameter Editing Alerts Demo plan
Phase 05 — Validation
Testing Against Real Operational Scenarios
Prototypes were tested with 8 supply chain managers across 3 companies. Key learnings led to the inline edit pattern replacing modal forms, the archive-before-delete safety flow, and the "Save as New Plan" fork pattern for shared plans. Final handoff included annotated specs, component documentation, and the design system for the engineering team.

Design Decisions

Why We Designed It This Way

Planner's interface had to make complex simulation output immediately readable — and editable — without hiding the precision that brand owners depend on. These four decisions shaped how.

Decision 01

Freshtime Points as a Simulation Currency

Rather than exposing raw Q10 sensitivity values and decay equations, we introduced Freshtime Points as the simulation's output language. Each product starts at 100 points. Each business step consumes points based on the time and temperature it applies. The running total is compared against predefined quality thresholds — so teams can see budget compliance at a glance.

The calculation is handled by the backend. The UI surfaces the result. This lets brand owners make proactive decisions about whether a trip is viable without needing to understand the underlying kinetics model.

"1 Freshtime Point = 0.4 Ambient Hours. The model runs silently — users see remaining budget and threshold status, not equations."

Simulation Abstraction Budget Metaphor Threshold Compliance
Decision 02

Inline Editing with Live Budget Recalculation

For simulation to be useful, users must be able to adjust step parameters — temperature, duration — and immediately see how the change affects cumulative freshness consumption. Early modal-based editing broke this loop: the plan context disappeared and users couldn't observe the budget impact in real time.

Inline editing keeps the full plan visible while a row is active. Orange highlighting signals the editable state. Points Remaining recalculates live as values change — making the simulation genuinely interactive rather than a static report.

"Change 45°F → 38°F on the store step and watch Points Remaining update across every subsequent row. The plan recalculates without a page refresh."

Live Simulation Inline Editing Context Preservation
Decision 03

Three-Way Save for Scenario Comparison

Brand teams don't just need one validated plan — they need to compare scenarios. The three-way save bar (Update Current Plan · Save as New Plan · Cancel) makes scenario branching a first-class action. Teams can modify a step, save as a new plan, and directly compare simulations side by side without losing the original baseline.

When a shared plan is open, Update is hidden and a permissions banner explains why — keeping team collaboration clean without requiring a separate permissions settings flow.

"UPDATE · SAVE · CANCEL on a single bar. Scenario branching is one click away from any simulation state."

Scenario Branching Collaboration Explicit Save Actions
Decision 04

Archive Before Delete — Protecting Simulation History

Validated plans are operational records — they represent a trip that was tested and approved before execution. Deleting one by accident could erase a baseline that a team spent time building. The archive-before-delete flow adds structural friction: plans are archived first, and permanent deletion is only available from the dedicated Archived Plans page.

Color semantics reinforce the hierarchy: teal for reversible actions (Archive, Unarchive), red only for permanently destructive ones (Delete). Users read the consequence from color before reading the label.

"DELETE appears only on the Archived Plans page. No validated simulation baseline is lost by accident."

Simulation Record Safety Destructive Action Hierarchy Color Semantics

Edge Cases & Error States Designed: The simulation must be trustworthy. Validation states (L5 threshold violating L4 ordering), permission barriers (shared plan read-only with fork path), empty states (no plans loaded, no product selected), and unit-mismatch warnings when accepting plans from users with different temperature unit settings were all explicitly designed — not left to fallback behaviour.

Validation
Life Alert Ordering

When L5 ≥ L4, the L5 input turns red and "L5 should be less than L4" appears. The save action is blocked until corrected. All other fields remain editable.

Permission
Read-Only Shared Plan

A persistent banner replaces the normal save bar: "This plan cannot be updated as it is created by other member." UPDATE is hidden; only SAVE (as new) is available.

Info
Unit Mismatch on Share

When accepting a plan from a user with different temp units, a notice explains: "The units of the shared items are kept as they are (change)" with a link to update preferences.

Warning
Critical Temperature

A 🔥 icon appears next to any sub-step where temperature exceeds the product's critical threshold (≥ 12.65°C for oysters), alerting to accelerated freshness loss.

State
Cancel Edit Confirmation

The three-way banner (UPDATE · SAVE · CANCEL) makes the cancel action explicit. CANCEL is always visible, styled in ghost/neutral to clearly signal it discards changes.

Destructive
Archive Before Delete

DELETE only appears in the Archived Plans page — never alongside active plans. Unarchive and Delete are always presented together so recovery is always the first option seen.

Results & Learnings

Outcomes & Reflections

Designing a simulation tool for pre-execution decision-making required rethinking what "useful output" means in a high-stakes operational context. Here's what the design delivered and what we learned.

17
Screens designed across all simulation and management flows
3
Business step types modeled — harvest, deliver, process
5
Configurable quality alert thresholds per plan simulation
9
Products preloaded in guest mode for immediate simulation

🧪 The Simulation Output Must Be Simpler Than the Model

The underlying freshness calculation involves Q10 kinetics, reference temperatures, and cumulative exposure. None of that belongs in the UI. Freshtime Points gave brand owners one number to track — their remaining freshness budget — making a complex backend model feel immediately actionable.

⚡ Live Recalculation Is Non-Negotiable for Simulation Tools

When users adjust a step parameter, the simulation must update immediately across the entire plan. Any delay or page reload breaks the hypothesis-testing loop that makes simulation valuable. Inline editing with live point recalculation was the most technically important design decision in the project.

🛡️ Validated Plans Are Records, Not Just Files

A plan that has been validated against quality thresholds represents a pre-execution approval. Losing it by accident is a business risk. The archive-before-delete structure treats plans as operational records with appropriate protection, not temporary workspace files.

📊 Scenario Branching Needs to Be One Click Away

Brand owners don't validate one scenario — they compare several. The three-way save bar made forking a simulation into a new scenario a single deliberate action from anywhere in the editing flow, rather than a multi-step process buried in plan management.

🌡️ Preloaded Data Removes the Biggest Barrier to Trial

The guest demo mode works because the simulation is already populated with real operational data. Prospective users experience genuine output — real decay curves, real point calculations, real threshold comparisons — without needing to configure anything. The product validates itself.

🔗 Team Simulation Requires Clear Ownership Boundaries

When teams share plans, each member needs to be able to run their own simulations without risking the shared baseline. The read-only + fork pattern separated viewing from modifying at the data level — not just at the UI level — keeping collaborative simulation safe.

Design Rationale
Why Let Guests Run a Full Simulation?

Planner's value proposition — proactive freshness validation — is abstract until you see it working. A feature list doesn't communicate what it feels like to adjust a storage step and watch the remaining freshness budget update across the whole plan in real time.

The guest demo is full-fidelity: real operational data, real decay calculations, real threshold comparisons. Prospective brand owners experience the simulation output before they sign up — which is far more persuasive than any marketing copy.

→ Preloaded data removes all setup friction — simulation runs immediately
→ 9 products covering food, pharma, and perishables — broad enough to feel relevant
→ Full chart access — the decay curve is the most compelling output
→ Sidebar scoped to 5 items — no confusion from locked-behind-login features
